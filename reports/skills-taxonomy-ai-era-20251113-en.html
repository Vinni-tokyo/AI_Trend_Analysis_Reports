Top 5 Trends

1. Global Skills Classification Standards: From 33,000 to 35 Skills

Overview: The global labor market operates with three major skills classification systems, each defining talent capabilities with different granularity and coverage.

Lightcast Skills Taxonomy

  • Scale: 33,000+ skills, 3-tier hierarchy
  • Data Sources: 1B+ job postings, hundreds of millions of profiles
  • Update Frequency: Real-time updates every 2 weeks
  • Features: Regional supply-demand analysis, 40,000+ data sources

O*NET¬∑ESCO Standards

  • O*NET: 35 overarching skills, 923 occupations (US Government)
  • ESCO: 13,890 occupation profiles, 3,008 skills (EU, 27 languages)
  • Methodology: Statistical random sampling, business & worker surveys
  • Use Cases: Policy making, education curricula, labor market analysis

2. AI-Powered Skills Intelligence Platforms: From Static Taxonomy to Dynamic Ontology

Overview: The evolution from traditional skills taxonomies to skills ontologies that capture complex relationships is accelerating.

Major Platforms

  • Eightfold AI: Machine learning-based skill relationship inference
  • iMocha: Primary and secondary skill mapping for 3,000+ roles

Advantages of Ontology

  • Relationship Mapping: Define dependencies and prerequisites between skills
  • Real-Time Updates: Dynamic adjustments aligned with industry trends
  • Talent Matching: Advanced workforce placement and career path design
  • Big Data Utilization: Infer competencies from performance and learning data

Key Terminology

Skills Taxonomy - Hierarchical framework systematically classifying and organizing skills. Primarily used for recruitment, workforce planning, and learning programs
Skills Ontology - Structured framework defining complex relationships between skills (dependencies, prerequisites, similarities). Used in AI-driven systems and advanced talent matching
Skills Intelligence Platform - AI and big data analytics systems that understand, track, and manage workforce capabilities in real-time. Enable current skill identification, future needs prediction, and gap mapping
O*NET (Occupational Information Network) - Free occupational information database operated by US Department of Labor. Defines 923 occupations and 35 overarching skills. Periodically updated via statistical sampling
ESCO (European Skills, Competences, Qualifications and Occupations) - Multilingual occupation classification system for EU labor markets. Supports 27 languages, includes 13,890 occupation profiles and 3,008 skills
Lightcast Skills Taxonomy - Open-source library of 33,000+ skills. Collected from 1B job postings and hundreds of millions of profiles, updated every 2 weeks. Features regional supply-demand analysis
Skills-Based Hiring - Recruitment approach prioritizing actual skills and competencies over educational background and work history. Mainstreamed as IBM, Google, Microsoft removed degree requirements
KANVAS (Kolmogorov-Arnold Network Versatile Algorithmic Solution) - Labor market classification system using Kolmogorov-Arnold Networks. Distinguishes traditional AI roles from GenAI-related positions using explainable machine learning
